## Primary Research

#### Interviews

###### Set 1 Findings (19-23y/o persons who have migrated as children and have grown up in a new state/ have spent childhood as migrants)
- The main motivators for learning languages are Academic, Pragmatic and Social.
- Daily interaction with local language speakers is most effective.
- Practicing with Parents & Friends can encourage with motivation and build familiarity
- Languages are really necessary to build connections with friends at schools(This differs among CBSE, ICSE and state schools) In all cases they make it easier none the less.
- Schooling can help with reading and writing but without any daily speaking practice, speaking isn't tackled.
- Every-day phrases is a really good starting point for language learning.




###### Set 2 Findings (Parents and children 8-15, families moved to Bengaluru from other states)
- Parents trust teachers more than apps, although some parent see sending children to additional classes as a hassle.
- In younger children parents are worried about children not being able to make new friends, where as in slightly older children academics takes precedent.
- Most parents and children have tried kannada learning apps but never end up sticking to them.
- Children don't see learning languages as a priority.
- Children see language learning apps and classes a boring as it tarts to feel repetitive.
- Apps make the child practice in isolation which can sow the pace of learning languages.
- Classes can lead to distractions by friends and can lead to fatigue and drop in energy levels.
- Children tend to develop a complex with regard to kannada as a language as it seems out of reach.

|**Aspect**|**Challenge/Feedback**|**Opportunity/Strategy**|
|---|---|---|
|**Academic Integration**|Remedial classes are isolated from the academic curriculum, making it difficult to correlate school progress with language fluency.|Develop an integrated curriculum that ties classroom learning with everyday language usage, reinforcing both academic content and language skills.|
|**Emotional & Social Barriers**|Students feel nervous, shy, and embarrassed when speaking Kannada, often comparing themselves to peers who have been studying longer, impacting self-esteem.|Incorporate gamified, low-pressure speaking exercises and community features that celebrate progress to boost confidence and reduce anxiety.|
|**Community & Peer Learning**|Difficulty in finding Kannada-speaking friends; existing apps lack features that promote a sense of community and peer support.|Implement peer-to-peer interactions, group challenges, discussion forums, and local meetup events to foster a supportive learning community.|
|**Real-World Context**|Migrants need practical language skills for navigating daily life, such as interacting in local cultural contexts, which current methods don’t address.|Embed real-world scenarios and modules (e.g., directions, ordering food, cultural references) to build everyday language proficiency and confidence.|
|**Parental & Teacher Collaboration**|Communication gaps exist between teachers and parents regarding a child’s language progress and learning methods.|Create shared progress dashboards, regular feedback loops, and resources that help align learning goals and strategies between teachers and parents.|
|**Learning Fatigue**|Extra remedial classes increase fatigue and reduce student engagement.|Offer micro-learning sessions and short, interactive activities that integrate seamlessly into busy schedules, minimizing overload.|
|**App Overwhelm**|Families are confused by the multitude of app options available, making it challenging to choose the right one.|Position the platform as an all-in-one, user-friendly solution with clear progression pathways that cater specifically to the needs of migrant families.|











## User Persona


## User Journey: Jay Joshi (11 years old)

**Phase 1: Confronting the Mandate**

- **Initial Exposure:**
    - Jay, comfortable with Hindi, suddenly faces a school mandate to study Kannada using traditional, rote-based methods.
    - The textbooks and lesson plans feel outdated leaving him disengaged.
    - Class sessions focus on memorizing vocabulary, making the learning process feel mechanical.

**Phase 2: Classroom Struggles & Social Pressure**

- **Group Dynamics:**
    - Peers who have grown up with Kannada sometimes tease him for mispronunciations, intensifying his feelings of inadequacy.

**Phase 3: Home & Community Reinforcement That Falls Short**

- **Parental Efforts:**
    - Although his parents try to help with Kannada storybooks and casual practice, the materials are not engaging or adapted to his age, and they lack the cultural context that resonates with him.
    - Family outings to local markets expose him to everyday Kannada.
- **Internal Conflict:**
    - As he struggles in both school, Jay begins to internalize a sense of failure, gradually developing a mild language complex—he starts believing that he isn’t smart enough to master Kannada.

**Phase 4: Long-Term Impact**

- **Academic Withdrawal:**
    - The persistent lack of relatable teaching methods and supportive feedback leads Jay to withdraw from class participation and group activities, reinforcing his isolation.

## User Journey: Parvati Iyer (13 years old)

**Phase 1: Early Exposure & Cultural Disconnect**

- **Initial Classroom Experience:**
    - **Context:** After moving to Bengaluru, Parvati learns Kannada through a curriculum that emphasizes formal language, grammar, and literature.
    - The course does not cover the colloquial expressions or local idioms essential for everyday conversation.
    - She often hears classmates laughing and exchanging cultural jokes that use nuances absent from textbooks, making her feel perpetually out of the loop.

**Phase 2: Social Exclusion & Insufficient Guidance**

- **Peer Interactions:**
    - When Parvati asks for help understanding local jokes or idioms, her questions are met with impatient responses or teasing, deepening her sense of alienation.
    - In casual settings like lunch breaks, her attempts to join in conversations are stifled by a language barrier that no formal class seems to address.
- **Teacher Support Gap:**
    - Teachers, focused on academic achievement, rarely facilitate discussions that bridge formal Kannada with everyday speech, leaving a crucial gap in her learning.

**Phase 3: Attempts at Cultural Immersion That Fall Short**

- **Community Engagement:**
    - Parvati attends local festivals and community events hoping to learn from real-life interactions, but the rapid, idiomatic Kannada used there overwhelms her.
    - Even in cultural workshops, the pace and depth of local humor and expressions remain inaccessible, reinforcing her feeling of being an outsider.
- **Emotional Impact:**
    - The persistent inability to decode cultural nuances leads Parvati to develop a subtle inferiority complex, questioning whether she will ever truly belong.

**Phase 4: Lingering Self-Doubt & Social Withdrawal**

- **Erosion of Confidence:**
    - Without a structured bridge between formal instruction and the informal language of daily life, Parvati’s self-confidence erodes over time.
    - She starts avoiding group conversations and social gatherings, further isolating herself and slowing her overall language acquisition.

## User Journey: Zaki Khan (15 years old)

**Phase 1: Overwhelming Classroom Environment**

- **Initial Struggles:**
    - **Context:** Zaki, new to Bengaluru from Amritsar, enters Kannada classes that assume a baseline knowledge he simply doesn’t have.
    - The rapid pace and rote-based instruction do not accommodate his slower, more thoughtful learning style, leaving him lost from the start.
    - Class discussions move quickly, and his repeated requests for clarification go unanswered or are met with curt remarks, increasing his anxiety.

**Phase 2: Isolation & Inadequate Remediation**

- **Limited Individual Attention:**
    - The school’s remedial support is generic, failing to offer the personalized, one-on-one guidance Zaki needs.
    - In large classes, he finds himself invisible—his struggles unnoticed by overburdened teachers, who assume he will catch up on his own.
- **Emotional Toll:**
    - Repeated failures and a lack of effective intervention lead Zaki to develop a deep-seated language complex, where he internalizes his difficulties as personal shortcomings.
    - His hesitation to ask questions becomes a self-reinforcing cycle, pushing him further into silence and isolation.

**Phase 3: Social Anxiety & Escalating Self-Doubt**

- **Peer Dynamics:**
    - In group settings, Zaki’s hesitant participation often attracts pity or subtle ridicule from classmates, which reinforces his sense of inferiority.
    - Every mispronunciation or slow response becomes a reminder of his perceived inadequacy, intensifying his reluctance to engage.
- **Lack of Tailored Interventions:**
    - The school’s failure to provide an environment that respects his pace or addresses his emotional needs leaves him feeling trapped.

**Phase 4: Long-Term Consequences**

- **Deepening Isolation:**
    - Without targeted, empathetic intervention, Zaki’s academic performance in Kannada continues to lag, and his self-esteem takes a further hit.
    - Over time, the cumulative effects of isolation, a mismatch between his learning needs and available support, and the negative social feedback contribute to a persistent belief that he will never overcome the language barrier.

### 1️⃣ **Kannada Alphabets (Aksharagalu)**

Kannada script alli 49 aksharagalu ide:

- **Vowels (Swaragalu)** - 13
- **Consonants (Vyanjanagalu)** - 34
- **Ottakshara (Conjunct letters)** - Mix of letters

**Examples:**

- ಅ (a), ಆ (aa), ಇ (i), ಈ (ii), ಉ (u), ಊ (uu)
- ಕ (ka), ಖ (kha), ಗ (ga), ಘ (gha), ಙ (nga)

### 2️⃣ **Basic Kannada Words**

|English|Kannada (Latin)|Kannada (Script)|
|---|---|---|
|Hello|Namaskara|ನಮಸ್ಕಾರ|
|How are you?|Neevu hegiddira?|ನೀವು ಹೇಗಿದ್ದೀರ?|
|I am fine|Naanu channagiddini|ನಾನು ಚೆನ್ನಾಗಿದ್ದೇನೆ|
|Thank you|Dhanyavada|ಧನ್ಯವಾದ|

### 3️⃣ **Simple Sentences**

- **Ninna hesarenu?** (What is your name?) – ನಿನ್ನ ಹೆಸರು ಏನು?
- **Naanu coffee ishta padthini.** (I like coffee.) – ನಾನು ಕಾಫಿ ಇಷ್ಟಪಡುತ್ತೇನೆ.
- **Ivattu chennagide.** (Today is good.) – ಇವತ್ತು ಚೆನ್ನಾಗಿದೆ.
